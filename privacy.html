Privacy & Governance

How surveillance data are protected, shared, and communicated

This page addresses the major communication concerns: privacy, data sharing, trust, and federal-provincial collaboration.

🔒 Privacy protection

Public pages would only display aggregated and anonymized results. Individual patient information, farm-level information, and identifiable laboratory records would not be publicly displayed.

The purpose of the public portal is to communicate trends, not to provide access to raw surveillance data.

Data access model

Public users: dashboards, summaries, trends, and reports.

Authorized stakeholders: more detailed datasets through protected access.

Data contributors: reporting tools and feedback on data quality.

Federal-provincial collaboration

The portal would not replace provincial or territorial surveillance programs. Instead, PHAC would coordinate national reporting standards while provinces and territories continue collecting and managing data within their own jurisdictions.

Governance and accountability

Data-sharing agreements, including expanded MLISA-style agreements, would define responsibilities for reporting, privacy, access, quality assurance, and use of surveillance information.

Why this communication strategy is useful

A Government of Canada website is appropriate because the policy is centered on surveillance and data integration. The website provides a trusted, centralized, and regularly updated platform for communicating surveillance findings, explaining privacy protections, and encouraging stakeholder participation.
